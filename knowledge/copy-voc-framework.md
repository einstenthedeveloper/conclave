# Voice of Customer (VOC) Framework for Conversion Copy
> Domain: Conversion Copywriting | Applies to: Performance Copywriter, CMO, Traffic Manager
> Status: stub | Created: 2026-04-25
> Primary source: Joanna Wiebe / CopyHackers VOC methodology; Conversion Copy Co.; CXL

---

## Purpose

Voice-of-Customer research is the process of extracting the exact language prospects and customers use to describe their pain, desire, trigger, and objections — and using that language verbatim (or near-verbatim) in copy. The premise: if a phrase appears in a copy document but not in any VOC data source, it has not earned its place. This document covers: (1) VOC mining sources, (2) data categorization taxonomy, (3) minimum sample rule, (4) awareness ladder classification, and (5) sticky phrase extraction.

---

## Why VOC Exists in This Protocol

The documented failure mode: copywriters write from assumption about what the ICP cares about. The copy may be grammatically correct and structurally sound but uses vocabulary, pain framing, or aspiration language that does not match the ICP's internal monologue. Evidence: the GitPrime case study (Conversion Copy Co.) — original copy addressed a problem executives didn't recognize. After VOC research on sales calls, copy was reframed around problems the audience actually articulated.

The rule from Joanna Wiebe's methodology: "Respected conversion copywriters don't write copy — they swipe sticky copy from Voice-of-Customer research." The copywriter's job is pattern recognition and selection, not invention.

## VOC Mining Sources

### Tier 1 — Highest fidelity (exact customer language)

| Source | What to mine | How |
|---|---|---|
| Customer interview transcripts | Pain statements, desire statements, trigger moments, exact phrases used to describe the decision | Read transcript or listen to recording; highlight exact quotes, do not paraphrase |
| Sales call recordings (Gong, Chorus, manual) | Objections raised, language used to describe the problem, how they described their current solution's failure | Search for phrases: "the reason I" / "the problem is" / "what I really want" / "what I hate about" |
| Support tickets and chat logs | Recurring pain language, how customers describe failure states | Export last 90 days; sort by issue type; find recurring exact phrases |
| NPS / CSAT open-text responses | Language of satisfied customers (why they stay) and dissatisfied customers (what's missing) | Filter for verbatim responses; ignore ratings-only entries |
| Customer onboarding / success call notes | What customers said they wanted when they first signed up vs. what they actually use | Ask CSM for first-call notes or review CRM notes fields |

### Tier 2 — High fidelity (proxy customer language)

| Source | What to mine | How |
|---|---|---|
| G2 / Capterra / Trustpilot reviews (your product or direct competitors) | Pain before switching, desired outcomes, language used to describe value received | Search reviews by star rating: 4–5 star = desire + outcome; 2–3 star = objections + unmet needs; 1 star = worst pain points |
| Amazon reviews (adjacent products — books, tools, courses) | Pain language, aspiration language, objection language from buyers of related category | Search category + relevant keyword; read 3-5 star reviews for desire statements; 1-2 star for complaints |
| Reddit threads (ICP's communities) | Unfiltered frustration language, how the problem is described to peers, alternative solutions considered | Search: "[ICP role] + [problem domain]"; look for posts that begin with "I'm so frustrated" / "anyone else deal with" / "best way to" |
| Quora / LinkedIn / Facebook Group posts | Questions asked about the problem domain — indicate problem awareness level | Questions = problem-aware language; answers = solution language |
| Competitor landing page copy (high-converting pages) | Vocabulary that is already resonating with a shared audience | Note which phrases appear above the fold and in CTAs on top-performing competitor pages |

### Tier 3 — Contextual (useful but lower direct fidelity)

| Source | What to mine | How |
|---|---|---|
| Industry analyst reports (Gartner, Forrester, McKinsey) | Framing language used in B2B buying decisions; how executives describe category problems | Look for quote sections from CXO interviews |
| Job postings for ICP roles | Language used to describe the job's pain points, goals, and tools — written by and for the ICP | Search LinkedIn/Indeed for [ICP role]; read the "About the Role" sections; they describe the day-to-day problems |
| Podcast / YouTube transcript search | Long-form discussion of the problem domain by recognized practitioners | Use transcript search tools or subtitles; mine for specific complaint language |

## Data Categorization Taxonomy

Every VOC phrase is categorized on intake into one of four buckets:

| Bucket | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Pain** | A problem, frustration, or obstacle the ICP is experiencing right now | "I spend half my week chasing down status updates that should be automated." |
| **Desire** | An outcome or state the ICP wants to achieve or maintain | "I want to be the person on my team who always knows what's coming before anyone else does." |
| **Trigger** | The specific event or moment that made the ICP start looking for a solution | "After we missed our Q3 target because of a deal that fell apart in the last week, I started looking for something." |
| **Objection** | A reason the ICP gives for not yet purchasing or not believing the claim | "I've tried tools like this before and they don't actually get used after the first month." |

Each phrase is tagged with:
- Source (source type from table above + specific URL or identifier)
- Awareness stage (see Awareness Ladder below)
- Star rating if from review site

## Minimum Sample Rule

**Minimum 15 VOC data points before first draft.** A copy brief with fewer than 15 phrases is not a complete brief — it is an incomplete research phase.

Why 15: below 15, the phrases collected may reflect one loud individual rather than a pattern. At 15+, recurring language starts to emerge — phrases that appear multiple times across multiple sources are the candidates for headlines and hooks. These are called "sticky phrases."

**Sticky phrase identification rule**: if the same phrase (or a close variant) appears 3+ times across 2+ independent sources, it is a sticky phrase. Sticky phrases are the highest-priority candidates for verbatim or near-verbatim use in copy.

**Sample distribution guidelines:**
- At least 5 phrases from Tier 1 sources (if available — for pre-launch products, Tier 2 is acceptable)
- At least 3 phrases per bucket (pain / desire / trigger / objection)
- At least 2 sources represented (not all from one review site or one customer)

## Awareness Ladder Classification (Eugene Schwartz)

Each VOC phrase also reveals the awareness stage of the person who wrote it. This determines which copy position (TOFU/MOFU/BOFU) the phrase supports.

| Stage | How to identify it in VOC | What it tells you about copy |
|---|---|---|
| **Unaware** | No mention of the problem or solutions — phrases about general business context | Copy must start with emotion or story, not the problem name |
| **Problem-Aware** | Describes the pain specifically but doesn't mention solutions or categories | Copy leads with pain validation; does not mention product category too early |
| **Solution-Aware** | Mentions solution categories or alternatives tried, but no specific product | Copy positions your approach vs. alternatives; leads with differentiation |
| **Product-Aware** | Mentions your product or direct competitors by name | Copy shifts to proof, specificity, and objection handling |
| **Most Aware** | Ready to buy but looking for the right trigger | Copy leads with offer, guarantee, or urgency — minimal education needed |

**Application**: the primary target awareness stage for a campaign is determined by where the majority of VOC data sits. If 10 of 15 phrases are from Problem-Aware sources, the landing page headline leads with pain validation — not the product name.

## Sticky Phrase Extraction Process

1. Complete data collection — minimum 15 phrases catalogued by source, bucket, and awareness stage.
2. Highlight phrases that appear 3+ times (or semantic variants) across 2+ sources → these are sticky phrase candidates.
3. For each sticky phrase, note: (a) exact original phrasing, (b) awareness stage, (c) which bucket it belongs to.
4. Rank sticky phrases by: frequency of occurrence (primary) × emotional intensity of language (secondary).
5. Top 5–7 sticky phrases are carried directly into the copy brief. These are the phrases that will appear verbatim or near-verbatim in headlines, hooks, subject lines, or testimonial selection criteria.

**Example sticky phrase extraction:**

| Phrase | Source count | Occurrences | Bucket | Stage |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| "I never know where a deal stands until it's too late" | 3 sources | 7 | Pain | Problem-Aware |
| "my CRM is more for reporting than actually helping me sell" | 2 sources | 4 | Pain | Solution-Aware |
| "I want to see around corners" | 2 sources | 3 | Desire | Solution-Aware |

The first phrase becomes the headline candidate: "You'll never wonder where a deal stands again." The VOC source language ("until it's too late") is preserved in the hook's emotional register.

## VOC Research Log Format

For every copy engagement, the research phase produces a VOC log section in `copy_brief_[campaign].md`:

```
## VOC Data Log

| # | Exact phrase | Source type | Source URL/ID | Bucket | Awareness stage | Sticky? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "[exact phrase]" | G2 review, 4-star | g2.com/[product]/reviews#[id] | Pain | Problem-Aware | No |
| 2 | "[exact phrase]" | Reddit r/[community] | reddit.com/r/[...] | Desire | Solution-Aware | Yes (3×) |
[...]

## Sticky Phrases (top 5–7)
1. "[phrase]" — Bucket: [Pain/Desire/Trigger/Objection] — Appeared: [N]× across [M] sources
[...]

## Awareness Stage Assessment
Based on VOC data: [N]% of phrases are at [stage] level.
Dominant stage: [stage]
Copy position: [TOFU / MOFU / BOFU]
```

## Common VOC Research Failures

1. **Paraphrasing instead of quoting**: copywriter rewords the customer's phrase before logging it. The paraphrase loses the exact emotional register and vocabulary. Fix: always log verbatim; paraphrase only in the headline draft phase, and document what the original phrase was.

2. **All data from one source**: 15 phrases from one review site represents one voice, not the market. Fix: enforce the 2+ source rule for sticky phrase qualification.

3. **Confusing aspirational copy with VOC**: reading a competitor's landing page and treating their marketing language as customer language. Competitor copy is not VOC — it is another copywriter's interpretation. VOC comes from customers speaking to other customers or in unprompted contexts.

4. **Skipping objection collection**: researching only pain and desire; no objections documented. Copy that doesn't address objections leaves the reader with unanswered resistance. Fix: minimum 3 objection phrases required before brief is locked.
