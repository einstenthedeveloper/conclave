# Positioning Framework (April Dunford — Obviously Awesome)

Positioning is the deliberate definition of the context in which a product is understood. Apply in order:

1. **Competitive alternatives** — what the customer actually compares this to (not what you think you compete with). Ask: "What would they do if this didn't exist?"
2. **Unique attributes** — what this product does that the alternatives cannot. Must be demonstrable, not claimed.
3. **Value those attributes produce** — the specific outcome the attribute delivers for this customer. Attribute → Value, not Attribute → Feature.
4. **Customer characteristics** — what makes a customer capable of receiving that value? Who has the context to appreciate it?
5. **Market category** — the frame that sets customer expectations before you say a word. Wrong category = constant uphill battle. Right category = customer arrives pre-educated.
6. **Relevant trends** — what makes this moment the right time? Trend must accelerate the value, not just co-exist with it.

**Positioning statement format:**
"For [ICP], [product] is the [market category] that [unique value] — unlike [competitive alternative]."

**Failure signals:**
- Positioning that describes features, not outcomes
- Market category chosen to sound impressive, not to set correct expectations
- Competitive alternative listed as a category ("spreadsheets") not a named product
- Value statement uses the word "better" without specifying better at what and for whom
