# High-Ticket / Luxury Acquisition Protocol

Products priced above $5,000 (B2B) or $1,000 (B2C) require a fundamentally different acquisition model. Volume-based digital acquisition (ads, SEO, content) does not work at high ticket — trust and specificity are the primary drivers.

**Why standard acquisition fails at high ticket:**
- Decision timeline is 30–90+ days (vs. minutes for low-ticket)
- Multiple stakeholders are involved (vs. single buyer)
- Trust signals must be extreme (vs. standard social proof)
- Price anchoring must be deliberate (vs. transparent pricing pages)

**High-ticket acquisition sequence:**

**1. Warm network first (weeks 1–8)**
Every high-ticket first sale comes from a relationship, not an ad. Map the founder's network for: (a) potential buyers who fit the ICP, (b) connectors who know potential buyers. Outreach must be personalized and specific — "I'm building X for people who have Y problem, which I know you deal with. Would a 20-minute conversation be valuable?" No pitch decks on first contact.

**2. Authority positioning (parallel with outreach)**
Publish 2–3 pieces of content that demonstrate deep expertise in the buyer's problem — not the product's features. The content must make the buyer think "this person understands my situation better than I do." Placement matters: where does the buyer read? Be there.

**3. Diagnostic conversation (not a sales call)**
Frame first meetings as diagnostic: "I'm going to ask you questions about your situation, and tell you honestly what I think would help — whether that's us or not." High-ticket buyers are trained to detect sales pressure. The diagnostic frame signals confidence and removes pressure simultaneously.

**4. Proposal as commitment mechanism**
Send proposals only to qualified buyers who have verbally confirmed the problem is real, the budget exists, and the timeline is active. Proposal = mutual commitment to evaluate seriously, not a brochure.

**5. Proof architecture**
High-ticket requires: (a) named case studies with specific outcomes (not "a Fortune 500 company"), (b) credentials that map to the buyer's context, (c) risk reversal (guarantee, pilot, milestone-based payment). Price anchoring: present highest tier first, then step down. Never present lowest tier first.
